- What is SCRUM and Agile? (per requested)
	- Business jargon for a way to **manage projects**
	- Focuses on **flexible, iterative approaches**
	- Teams work in **small manageable increments (sprints)** within a period of time
	- Emphasizes:
		- Customer **collaboration** and **continuous integration**
		- **Responding to change** over following a fixed plan
		- Submit in terms of **deliverables**
		- Individuals and **interactions** over tools and processes
	- Advantages:
		- Fast, prone to change
		- Best for projects with evolving or **uncertain requirements**
		- Product delivered in **small, usable increments**
		- **Repeated short cycles** of planning, building, and reviewing
	- Example: Launch a basic website quickly, then improve sections (UI, features, SEO) in short cycles based on analytics and feedback.

### 3. Prototype Design & Evaluation Plan
- **Coarse User Stories**
	- Key functionality of your proposed system
	- Core user stories for each stakeholder
	- Prioritise them using MoSCoW prioritisation (i.e. Must have, Should have, Could have, Won't have).
	
- **Low Fidelity Prototype**
	- Physical: Paper prototyping, cardboard
	- Web: Moqup, Balsamiq, Figma
	- You are not expected to do any programming, and you will lose marks if the fidelity of your prototype is inappropriate.
	- By definition, is not a polished design
	- Should not have obvious conceptual or usability problems
	- Appropriate consideration to how your prototype could be made accessible
	- Product should be:
		- Novel (Different from existing product)
		- Technically feasible incorporating technology which exists NOW
		- Must not rely on speculative “machine learning” and do more than just log data and display it to users
	- Aside from prototype itself, you should prepare a description of your prototype using text, sketches and diagrams to help others understand what you are proposing
	
- **Evaluation Plan**
	- Prepare a plan for evaluating your final, high-fidelity, prototype at the end of the project
	- Include a description of the evaluation method that you will use, and the participants that you will recruit.
	- Include the evaluation protocol including the tasks that participants will complete and the questions that they will be asked
	- Restrictions:
		- Participants in your evaluation must be from another COMP2300 group.
		- You must recruit the participants yourself (by finding another group and asking them to take part).
		- You must use a user-centred evaluation method (e.g. usability testing, think-aloud protocol, cognitive walkthrough, etc.)
		- The evaluation must take place in a lab setting (a quiet room where you can control the environment).
		- You must not collect or try to elicit any sensitive personal data (e.g. health data, sexual health data, political or religious beliefs, etc.) from participants.
	- A heuristic evaluation is NOT a user-centred evaluation method, and is not acceptable (by itself) for your final evaluation (although you could still add that)
	
- Submit **Single PDF**:
	- Deliverable report
	- Pictures/sketches/description of your prototype
